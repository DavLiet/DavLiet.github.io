About the Flesch Reading Ease Test

The Flesch Reading Ease Test is used to quantify how difficult it is to read a specific text based on a 100-point scale. Higher scores translate into easier to understand text passages, while lower scores mean that a passage is harder to read. Motivation for Visualization

To help people better understand what makes a text "difficult" to read, my visualization aims to educate people about the underlying factors that directly contribute to the overall reading difficulty of a given passage as calculated by the Flesch Reading Ease Test. Using a collection of fairy tales from The Project Gutenberg EBook of English Fairy Tales, my visualization aims to achieve this goal by accomplish the following tasks:

  1. show users the Flesch Reading Ease scores for all Fairy Tales given
  2. illustrate the different factors that influence the overall Flesh Reading Ease score that is calculated
    • Total Number of Words
    • Total Number of Sentences
    • Total Number of Syllables
    • Total Number of Complex Words, where a complex word is defined as having 3 or more syllables
  3. allow users to compare two stories in order to observe how the different readability scores are the result of different values of factors.

Because the Flesch Reading Ease equation calculates its score based off the average number of words per sentence and the average syllables per word, this means that larger values for certain factors don't necessarily correlate to lower Flesch Reading Ease scores. For example, just because one story has more total words than the other does not necessarily mean that it will be more difficult to read. Of the four factors that this visualization illustrates, you will notice that the total number of complex words is the only factor not present in the Flesch Reading Ease equation. This factor was included in this visualization because it still directly affects the overall score since a higher number of complex words will contribute to a higher number of total syllables, which increases the reading difficulty of a passage.

The color scale of this visualization is used to qualitatively represent the Flesch Readability Score of a fairy tale relative to the other fairy tales in this collection. For example, a fairy tale whose graph has the brightest color from the color scale is the easiest to read out of the collection of 43 fairy tales. This does not mean it's Flesch Readability Score is 0.
